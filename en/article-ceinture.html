The art of the belt —
shaping your silhouette with elegance

A belt does not merely hold a coat closed. It transforms a silhouette, declares a waist, metamorphoses a volume. Everything you need to know about the art of the belt.

By Aude & Mathilde · April 2026 · 8 min read

There are coats that you wear and coats that wear you. The difference is rarely in the fabric or the cut alone. Often it is in a detail: the way the belt is tied. Too tight, too loose, left dangling or wrongly knotted, and the most beautiful piece can look shapeless. Mastered, that same belt makes the silhouette. This guide is about the gesture that changes everything.

What a belt actually does to a coat

A belt at the waist does three things simultaneously. It defines the waist on a piece that covers it completely. It controls the volume of a fabric — particularly important with soft, draping materials like cachemire. And it signals an intention: a tied belt says that you are not simply protected against the cold, but that you have dressed with purpose.

This is why a simple change in how you tie your belt can give a completely different impression of the same coat: more casual, more structured, more dramatic, more understated. Mastering a few tying techniques means multiplying the wardrobe value of a single piece.

« A well-tied belt is not an accessory. It is the punctuation of an outfit — the gesture that transforms a coat into a silhouette. »

Belt width: what it does to your proportions

Narrow belt (2–3 cm)

The narrow belt is discreet, almost delicate. It suggests the waist without imposing it — ideal for slim silhouettes who want to preserve the fluid, uninterrupted line of the coat. It suits perfectly with a long coat (midi or maxi) that you want to wear with a sense of lightness. It can be tied simply, with a small knot at the front, or left to hang slightly asymmetrically for a more casual feel.

Mid-width belt (4–5 cm)

The most versatile width. Wide enough to structure the waist without appearing costume-like, slim enough to remain elegant in the most diverse contexts. A mid-width belt suits almost all morphologies and all coat lengths. It is the width of the belts on the Yūn Long and the Yūn Court — not by chance.

Wide belt (6 cm and over)

The wide belt is a gesture in itself. It structures the waist with authority, creates an hourglass effect even on straighter silhouettes, and gives a coat almost sculptural dimension. It is the choice of those who want to make a statement — for a special occasion, an evening, or simply a day when you feel like it.

Four ways to tie your belt

1. The simple half-bow

The most classic and the most worn: you pass the belt around the waist, cross the two ends in front and make a simple bow. Soft, feminine, natural. It can be adjusted by pulling more or less on the loops depending on the effect you want: a wide bow for a romantic feel, a tight, compact bow for something more structured. The knot position matters too: perfectly centred reads formal; slightly to one side adds movement and character.

2. The double wrap

Pass the belt around the waist twice before tying it. This creates a layered, sculptural effect that amplifies the waist mark without the belt appearing too thin. It works particularly well with narrow to mid-width belts on long coats. The result is more graphic, more deliberate than the simple bow — a styling detail that often goes unnoticed but that those who know understand immediately.

3. The asymmetric side knot

Instead of tying the belt in the centre, bring both ends to one side and make a knot or simple bow there. This creates an asymmetric line that is dynamic without being aggressive. It works with both narrow and mid-width belts, and particularly suits those who find the classic central knot too neat. A variation: a simple knot (not a bow) on the side, letting one end hang longer — very fashion, perfectly controlled.

4. The bow with long hanging ends

A generous bow in the centre with ends long enough to fall naturally below the knot. More structured and visible than the simple bow, it creates a strong focal point at the waist. The length of the hanging ends matters: too short and it looks truncated, too long and it becomes unwieldy. Ideally the ends reach mid-thigh. This knot suits long coats best — it adds presence on an already generous silhouette.

Key principle: the knot should not be tied flat against the fabric. Always let the bow sit a little proud so it has volume and relief. A flat bow against the coat looks like it has been ironed — which kills all the effect.

Adapting the belt to your morphology

The belt is one of the most personalised elements of dressing, because it responds directly to each body's proportions. A few general principles — with the understanding that these are starting points, not rules.

  • Small build (under 1.62 m) — favour narrow to mid-width belts, tied high (just above the natural waist). Avoid oversized bows that break the vertical line. The double-wrap is particularly flattering as it adds structure without creating a horizontal cut at the hips.
  • Tall build (over 1.72 m) — you can allow yourself wide belts and generous bows. The long coat with an XL belt tied at natural waist height is the combination made for you — it creates a commanding, assertive presence.
  • Straight silhouette — the belt is your best ally. Position it at the slimmest part of your torso and tie it with a well-structured bow. A double wrap with a mid-width belt creates the waist definition that changes everything.
  • Hourglass silhouette — the belt can be worn looser, almost decorative. The waist already asserts itself naturally; the belt marks it without needing to artificially emphasise it. A simple knot is often enough.

Colour and material accords

When the belt is the same fabric as the coat — as is the case with Atelier MAUDÉ pieces — the question does not arise: it is a naturally harmonious ensemble. But if you use an additional belt, the accord deserves some thought.

The most reliable accord: a belt in the same tone as the coat but in a different material — a leather belt on a cachemire coat creates a textural contrast that is at once rich and understated. Choose a natural leather: tan, caramel, chocolate brown. Avoid shiny or plastic-effect belts on fine materials.

For a more deliberate contrast: a belt in an accent colour — burgundy on camel, black on off-white, dark brown on camel. Be careful that the accent colour appears elsewhere in your outfit (bag, shoes, scarf) so that the belt does not look isolated.

MAUDÉ belts: designed to be tied

Atelier MAUDÉ belts are not thought of as closures but as styling elements. Their length has been calculated to allow all tying methods described in this guide: simple bow, double wrap, asymmetric knot, bow with long ends. Their width — between 4 and 5 cm — is deliberately versatile.

They are made in the same cachemire and laine blend as the coats, which means they will age exactly the same way: gradually softening, developing a patina that reveals the material's quality rather than degrading it. After three winters, your belt will be more beautiful than on the first day. That is one of the privileges of wearing material that actually has substance.

The Yūn Long, Yūn Court, and Huā Long — all three with removable belts — are the direct application of this philosophy: a coat that can be worn open, half-open, loosely belted, or tightly structured depending on mood, weather, or occasion. The same piece, in its full versatility.
